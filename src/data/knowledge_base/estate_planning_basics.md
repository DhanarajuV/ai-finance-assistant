# Estate Planning Basics

Estate planning is not just for the wealthy. Everyone who has assets, dependents, or specific wishes about their medical care should have a basic estate plan in place.

## Why Everyone Needs a Plan

If you pass away without an estate plan, your state's intestacy laws dictate how your assets are distributed. This may not match your wishes:

- An unmarried partner may receive nothing
- Assets could be split in ways you did not intend
- Courts may appoint a guardian for your minor children that you would not have chosen

## Wills

A will is the foundation of most estate plans. It allows you to:

- Name who receives your property (beneficiaries)
- Appoint a guardian for minor children
- Choose an executor to manage the process of settling your estate

A will only takes effect after death and must go through probate. It does not cover assets with named beneficiaries, such as retirement accounts or life insurance policies.

## Beneficiary Designations

Certain accounts pass directly to a named beneficiary outside of your will:

- 401(k) and IRA accounts
- Life insurance policies
- Payable-on-death bank accounts
- Transfer-on-death brokerage accounts

**Review your beneficiary designations regularly**, especially after major life events like marriage, divorce, or the birth of a child. An outdated beneficiary form can override what your will says.

## Power of Attorney

A power of attorney (POA) authorizes someone to act on your behalf if you become unable to manage your own affairs:

- **Financial POA:** Grants authority to handle banking, investments, bill payments, and property transactions
- **Healthcare POA:** Grants authority to make medical decisions if you cannot communicate your wishes

Without a POA, your family may need to go through guardianship or conservatorship proceedings, which are time-consuming and expensive.

## Probate Basics

Probate is the legal process of validating a will and distributing assets:

- The executor files the will with the local probate court
- Creditors are notified and given time to make claims
- Remaining assets are distributed to beneficiaries
- The process can take several months to over a year

Some assets bypass probate entirely, including those with beneficiary designations, jointly held property, and assets in a trust.

## Getting Started

A basic estate plan typically includes:

1. A will
2. Beneficiary designations reviewed and up to date
3. A financial power of attorney
4. A healthcare power of attorney or advance directive

Many attorneys offer basic estate planning packages at a reasonable cost.
