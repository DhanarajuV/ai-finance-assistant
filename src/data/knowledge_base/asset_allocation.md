# Asset Allocation Basics

Asset allocation is the process of dividing your investment portfolio among different asset categories. It is widely considered the most important decision an investor makes, because how you split your money between stocks, bonds, and cash has a greater impact on long-term results than which specific investments you pick.

## The Core Asset Classes

### Stocks (Equities)
- **Role:** Growth engine of your portfolio
- **Risk:** High; prices can swing dramatically in the short term
- **Reward:** Historically the highest long-term returns (~10% annually for U.S. stocks)
- **Best for:** Long-term goals where you can ride out volatility

### Bonds (Fixed Income)
- **Role:** Stability and income
- **Risk:** Lower than stocks, but not risk-free
- **Reward:** Moderate returns (~4–6% historically)
- **Best for:** Reducing portfolio volatility and providing predictable income

### Cash and Cash Equivalents
- **Examples:** Savings accounts, money market funds, short-term Treasury bills
- **Risk:** Very low, but returns often fail to keep up with inflation
- **Best for:** Emergency funds and money needed within 1–2 years

## Age-Based Rules of Thumb

A common starting point is to subtract your age from 110 to determine your stock allocation:

- **Age 25:** 110 - 25 = 85% stocks, 15% bonds
- **Age 40:** 110 - 40 = 70% stocks, 30% bonds
- **Age 55:** 110 - 55 = 55% stocks, 45% bonds
- **Age 65:** 110 - 65 = 45% stocks, 55% bonds

These are rough guidelines, not rigid rules. Your actual allocation should reflect your risk tolerance, goals, and circumstances.

## Why Asset Allocation Matters

Different asset classes perform differently under various market conditions. When stocks fall sharply, bonds often hold steady or rise. By holding a mix, you smooth out the ride:

- A 100% stock portfolio lost about 37% in 2008
- A 60/40 stock/bond portfolio lost about 22% in the same year

The right allocation helps you **stay invested** through turbulent markets, which is critical for long-term success.

## Rebalancing

Over time, market movements cause your allocation to drift. If stocks have a great year, they may grow from 70% to 80% of your portfolio, leaving you with more risk than intended.

**Rebalancing** means selling some outperformers and buying more underperformers to return to your target. Common approaches:

- **Calendar-based:** Rebalance once or twice a year
- **Threshold-based:** Rebalance when an asset class drifts more than 5% from target

Rebalancing enforces a disciplined "buy low, sell high" behavior.

## Target-Date Funds

If managing your own allocation feels overwhelming, **target-date funds** do it for you. Pick a fund based on your expected retirement year (e.g., a 2055 fund), and it automatically starts stock-heavy, gradually shifts toward bonds, and handles all rebalancing internally.

Target-date funds are a solid "set it and forget it" option, especially for retirement accounts.
