# Financial Glossary (A-M)

A quick-reference guide to common financial terms. Bookmark this page and return to it whenever you encounter unfamiliar terminology.

---

## Alpha

Alpha measures an investment's performance relative to a benchmark index. A positive alpha means the investment outperformed the benchmark; a negative alpha means it underperformed. For example, if the S&P 500 returned 10% and your fund returned 12%, the fund's alpha is +2%.

## Beta

Beta measures how much an investment's price moves relative to the overall market. A beta of 1.0 means the investment moves in line with the market. A beta above 1.0 indicates higher volatility (e.g., beta of 1.5 means 50% more volatile), while a beta below 1.0 indicates lower volatility. Utility stocks often have low betas; tech stocks often have higher betas.

## Blue Chip

A blue chip stock is a share in a large, well-established, financially stable company with a history of reliable performance. These companies are typically industry leaders with strong balance sheets. Examples include Apple, Johnson & Johnson, and Coca-Cola. The term originates from poker, where blue chips hold the highest value.

## Capital Gain

A capital gain is the profit earned when you sell an investment for more than you paid for it. If you buy a stock at $50 and sell it at $70, your capital gain is $20 per share. Capital gains can be **short-term** (held less than one year, taxed as ordinary income) or **long-term** (held more than one year, taxed at lower rates).

## Correction

A correction is a decline of 10% or more in the price of a security, index, or market from its most recent peak. Corrections are a normal part of market cycles and occur roughly once a year on average. They are distinct from bear markets, which involve declines of 20% or more.

## Dividend Yield

Dividend yield is the annual dividend payment divided by the stock's current price, expressed as a percentage. If a stock pays $2 per share annually and trades at $50, its dividend yield is 4%. Dividend yield helps investors compare the income-generating potential of different stocks.

## EPS (Earnings Per Share)

EPS is a company's net profit divided by the number of outstanding shares. It tells you how much profit the company earns for each share of stock. For example, if a company earns $10 million and has 5 million shares outstanding, its EPS is $2.00. EPS is one of the most widely used metrics for evaluating a company's profitability.

## FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation)

The FDIC is a U.S. government agency that insures deposits at member banks up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank, per ownership category. If an FDIC-insured bank fails, depositors are guaranteed to recover their insured funds. The FDIC does not cover investments like stocks, bonds, or mutual funds.

## GDP (Gross Domestic Product)

GDP is the total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country during a specific period, usually a quarter or a year. It is the broadest measure of economic activity. When GDP is growing, the economy is expanding; when it contracts for two consecutive quarters, it is commonly referred to as a recession.

## Hedge

A hedge is an investment made to reduce the risk of adverse price movements in another asset. For example, an investor who owns stocks might buy put options as a hedge against a market decline. Hedging does not eliminate risk entirely — it reduces potential losses at the cost of some potential gains.

## IPO (Initial Public Offering)

An IPO is the process by which a private company offers shares to the public for the first time. It allows the company to raise capital from public investors. After the IPO, the company's shares trade on a stock exchange. IPOs can be exciting but are often volatile in their early trading days.

## Liquidity

Liquidity refers to how quickly and easily an asset can be converted to cash without significantly affecting its price. Cash is the most liquid asset. Publicly traded stocks are generally liquid because they can be sold quickly on an exchange. Real estate and private business interests are less liquid because selling them takes time and effort.

## Market Order

A market order is an instruction to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available current price. Market orders guarantee execution but not price — in fast-moving markets, the price you receive may differ from the last quoted price. For more price control, investors can use limit orders, which only execute at a specified price or better.
