# Margin Trading

Margin trading means borrowing money from your broker to buy investments. It allows you to control a larger position than your cash alone would permit. While this can amplify gains, it equally amplifies losses — making it one of the riskiest tools available to individual investors.

## How Margin Works

When you open a margin account, your broker lends you money using your existing investments as collateral. For example, if you have $10,000 in cash and your broker offers 2:1 margin, you can buy up to $20,000 worth of stock — $10,000 of your own money and $10,000 borrowed.

You pay interest on the borrowed amount for as long as the loan is outstanding, similar to any other loan.

## How Margin Amplifies Gains AND Losses

Consider this example with $10,000 of your own money and $10,000 borrowed on margin:

**If the stock rises 20%:**
- Your $20,000 position grows to $24,000.
- After repaying the $10,000 loan, you have $14,000.
- Your actual return on your $10,000 is **40%** (minus interest).

**If the stock falls 20%:**
- Your $20,000 position drops to $16,000.
- After repaying the $10,000 loan, you have $6,000.
- Your actual loss on your $10,000 is **40%** (plus interest).

The leverage works both ways. A 50% decline would wipe out your entire investment, and you would still owe interest on the borrowed funds.

## Margin Calls

A margin call occurs when the value of your account falls below the broker's required minimum, known as the maintenance margin (typically 25-30% of the total position value). When this happens:

- Your broker demands that you deposit additional cash or securities immediately.
- If you cannot meet the margin call, the broker can **sell your holdings without your permission** to bring the account back into compliance.
- These forced sales often happen at the worst possible time — during market declines — locking in steep losses.

## Regulations

- **Regulation T** — Set by the Federal Reserve, it requires investors to deposit at least 50% of the purchase price when buying on margin (the initial margin requirement).
- **FINRA Rules** — Require a minimum maintenance margin of 25%, though most brokers set their own higher thresholds (often 30-40%).
- **Pattern Day Trader Rule** — Accounts that make four or more day trades within five business days must maintain at least $25,000 in equity.

## Why Margin Is Risky for Beginners

- **Losses can exceed your initial investment.** Unlike buying stocks with cash, margin can leave you owing money.
- **Interest costs eat into returns.** Even if your trade is profitable, margin interest reduces your net gain.
- **Emotional pressure increases.** Watching leveraged losses mount can lead to panic selling at the worst time.
- **Forced liquidation removes control.** Margin calls take the decision out of your hands.

Most experienced investors recommend that beginners avoid margin trading until they have a solid understanding of markets and risk management.
