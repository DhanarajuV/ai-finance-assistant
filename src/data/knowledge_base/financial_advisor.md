# Working with a Financial Advisor

A financial advisor can help you create a plan, stay disciplined, and navigate complex situations. But not all advisors are the same, and understanding the differences helps you find the right fit.

## When to Hire a Financial Advisor

You may benefit from professional guidance if you:

- Are approaching retirement and need a withdrawal strategy
- Have a complex tax situation (stock options, rental income, business ownership)
- Received a large sum of money (inheritance, home sale, bonus)
- Are going through a major life change (marriage, divorce, death of a spouse)
- Feel overwhelmed and want a second opinion on your financial plan
- Simply prefer to delegate investment management

If your finances are straightforward and you are comfortable managing your own investments, you may not need an advisor — but a one-time consultation can still be valuable.

## Fiduciary vs. Suitability Standard

This is the most important distinction when choosing an advisor:

- **Fiduciary standard:** The advisor is legally required to act in your best interest. They must recommend what is best for you, even if it means earning less themselves.
- **Suitability standard:** The advisor only needs to recommend products that are "suitable" for you, even if a cheaper or better option exists. This standard allows for conflicts of interest.

Always ask: **"Are you a fiduciary at all times?"** Some advisors act as fiduciaries only part of the time, which can be confusing.

## Fee Structures

Advisors are compensated in several ways:

- **Assets under management (AUM):** Typically 0.75%–1.25% of your portfolio annually. The advisor's fee grows as your portfolio grows.
- **Flat fee:** A fixed annual or per-plan fee regardless of portfolio size. Common range is $1,000–$5,000 per year.
- **Hourly fee:** Pay for specific consultations, typically $150–$400 per hour.
- **Commission-based:** The advisor earns commissions on products sold to you. This model has the highest potential for conflicts of interest.

Fee-only advisors (who do not accept commissions) generally have fewer conflicts of interest than commission-based advisors.

## Robo-Advisors vs. Human Advisors

**Robo-advisors** are automated platforms that build and manage a diversified portfolio for you:

- Low fees, typically 0.25%–0.50% of AUM
- Automated rebalancing and tax-loss harvesting
- Best for straightforward investment management
- Limited personalized advice on complex topics

**Human advisors** provide comprehensive planning:

- Higher fees but broader scope (tax planning, estate planning, insurance review)
- Personalized guidance during emotional market periods
- Better suited for complex financial situations

Some firms offer hybrid models that combine automated investing with access to a human advisor.

## Questions to Ask a Potential Advisor

Before hiring an advisor, ask:

1. Are you a fiduciary at all times?
2. How are you compensated? Are there any commissions or referral fees?
3. What are your qualifications and certifications? (Look for CFP, CFA, or CPA)
4. What is your investment philosophy?
5. How often will we meet or communicate?
6. Can you provide references from current clients?
7. What is your minimum account size?

Take your time choosing an advisor. The right one can add significant value; the wrong one can cost you.
