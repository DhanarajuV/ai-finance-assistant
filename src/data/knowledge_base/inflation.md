# Understanding Inflation

Inflation is the gradual increase in the general price level of goods and services over time. When inflation rises, each dollar you hold buys a little less than it did before. Understanding inflation is essential for making smart financial decisions because it directly affects your purchasing power and the real value of your savings.

## How Inflation Erodes Purchasing Power

If a gallon of milk costs $4.00 today and inflation averages 3% per year, that same gallon will cost about $5.38 in ten years. Your dollar does not go as far.

This matters for savings. If you keep $10,000 in a checking account earning 0% interest while inflation runs at 3%:

| Year | Nominal Value | Real Purchasing Power |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| 0 | $10,000 | $10,000 |
| 10 | $10,000 | $7,441 |
| 20 | $10,000 | $5,537 |
| 30 | $10,000 | $4,120 |

After 30 years, your $10,000 buys less than half of what it does today even though the number in your account has not changed.

## Historical Average

In the United States, inflation has averaged roughly **3% per year** over the long term, as measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI). However, it varies significantly:

- **1970s–early 1980s:** Inflation spiked above 10%, driven by oil crises
- **1990s–2010s:** Inflation was relatively tame, often between 1.5% and 3%
- **2021–2022:** Inflation surged above 7% due to pandemic-related disruptions

The Federal Reserve targets an inflation rate of about 2% per year as a healthy level for the economy.

## Why Investing Matters to Beat Inflation

To maintain and grow your purchasing power, your money needs to earn a return that exceeds inflation:

- **Checking account (0.01%):** Loses purchasing power every year
- **Traditional savings account (0.5%):** Still falls behind inflation
- **High-yield savings account (4–5%):** Keeps pace or slightly ahead short-term
- **Bonds (~4–6% historically):** Generally keeps up with or modestly beats inflation
- **Stocks (~10% historically):** Significantly outpaces inflation over the long term

Keeping all your long-term savings in cash guarantees a slow loss of purchasing power.

## TIPS: Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities

**TIPS** are U.S. government bonds specifically designed to protect against inflation:

- The principal value adjusts with the CPI
- If inflation rises, your principal increases and interest payments grow
- If deflation occurs, you are guaranteed at least your original investment at maturity

TIPS are useful for conservative investors wanting inflation protection, retirees needing income to keep pace with prices, and diversifying a bond portfolio. You can buy TIPS directly from TreasuryDirect.gov or through TIPS mutual funds and ETFs.

## Key Takeaways

- Inflation means prices rise over time and your money buys less
- The historical U.S. average is roughly 3% per year
- Cash savings lose real value if they do not earn above the inflation rate
- Investing in stocks and bonds is essential for long-term purchasing power
- TIPS bonds offer a government-backed way to hedge against inflation
