# Understanding Market Capitalization

Market capitalization, or "market cap," represents the total market value of a company's outstanding shares of stock. It is one of the simplest and most common ways to measure a company's size.

## How Market Cap Is Calculated

**Market Cap = Current Stock Price x Total Shares Outstanding**

For example, if a company has 1 billion shares outstanding and each share trades at $50, the market cap is $50 billion. Market cap changes constantly as the stock price moves throughout the trading day.

## Size Categories

Companies are generally grouped into categories based on their market cap:

- **Large-cap:** $10 billion and above. Well-established companies with long track records like Apple, Microsoft, and Johnson & Johnson. They tend to be more stable but may grow more slowly.
- **Mid-cap:** $2 billion to $10 billion. Often in a growth phase, offering a balance between stability and growth potential.
- **Small-cap:** $300 million to $2 billion. May offer higher growth potential but come with greater risk and price volatility.
- **Micro-cap:** Below $300 million. The smallest publicly traded companies, carrying the highest risk with lower trading volume and less analyst coverage.

## What Market Cap Tells You

- **Company size:** A $500 billion company operates on a very different scale than a $2 billion company.
- **Risk profile:** Larger companies are generally considered less risky than smaller ones, though exceptions exist.
- **Investment style:** Growth-focused investors may lean toward small and mid-cap stocks, while conservative investors often prefer large-caps.
- **Index inclusion:** Major indexes like the S&P 500 have market cap requirements for inclusion.

## What Market Cap Does Not Tell You

- **It is not the company's total value.** Enterprise value, which includes debt and subtracts cash, gives a more complete picture.
- **A high market cap does not mean a high stock price.** A company with a $10 stock price and 10 billion shares has a $100 billion market cap. Price per share alone is meaningless without knowing the share count.
- **It does not reflect fundamentals.** Market cap is driven by investor sentiment and stock price, which can be disconnected from actual financial health.

## Why Market Cap Matters for Your Portfolio

Understanding market cap helps you diversify effectively:

- Large-caps may hold up better during downturns.
- Small-caps have historically outperformed over long periods but with more volatility.
- Mid-caps often offer a middle ground.

Many index funds and ETFs are organized by market cap, making it easy to target specific size categories in your portfolio.

## Key Takeaway

Market capitalization is a foundational concept for understanding how companies are sized and categorized. Use it alongside other metrics to build a diversified portfolio that matches your risk tolerance and investment goals.
