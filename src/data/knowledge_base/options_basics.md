# Options Basics

Options are financial contracts that give the buyer the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell an asset at a specific price before a certain date. They are powerful tools, but they carry significant risk, especially for beginners.

## Calls and Puts

There are two types of options:

- **Call Option** — Gives the holder the right to **buy** a stock at a specified price. You buy a call when you believe the stock price will go up.
- **Put Option** — Gives the holder the right to **sell** a stock at a specified price. You buy a put when you believe the stock price will go down.

Each options contract typically represents 100 shares of the underlying stock.

## Key Terminology

- **Strike Price** — The price at which the option holder can buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying stock.
- **Premium** — The price you pay to purchase the option contract. This is the maximum you can lose as a buyer.
- **Expiration Date** — The date by which the option must be exercised or it becomes worthless.
- **In the Money (ITM)** — A call is ITM when the stock price is above the strike price. A put is ITM when the stock price is below the strike price.
- **Out of the Money (OTM)** — The opposite of ITM. The option has no intrinsic value.
- **Underlying Asset** — The stock or security that the option contract is based on.

## A Simple Example

Suppose Stock XYZ trades at $50. You buy a call option with a $55 strike price, paying a $2 premium per share ($200 total for 100 shares). If XYZ rises to $65 before expiration, your option is worth $10 per share, giving you a profit of $800 ($1,000 value minus $200 premium). If XYZ stays below $55, your option expires worthless and you lose the $200 premium.

## Covered Calls: A Beginner-Friendly Strategy

A covered call involves owning 100 shares of a stock and selling a call option against those shares. You collect the premium as income. If the stock stays below the strike price, you keep both the shares and the premium. If the stock rises above the strike, your shares may be called away (sold) at the strike price.

Covered calls are considered one of the more conservative options strategies because you already own the underlying shares, limiting your downside compared to selling uncovered (naked) options.

## Why Beginners Should Be Cautious

- **Options can expire worthless.** Unlike stocks, which you can hold indefinitely, options have a ticking clock. Many options expire with zero value.
- **Leverage amplifies losses.** A small move in the wrong direction can wipe out your entire investment quickly.
- **Complexity increases risk.** Multi-leg strategies (spreads, straddles, iron condors) require deep understanding to manage properly.
- **Selling options can carry unlimited risk.** Selling naked calls, for example, exposes you to theoretically unlimited losses.

Most financial educators recommend that beginners master stock investing fundamentals before exploring options.
