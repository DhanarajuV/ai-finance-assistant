# Saving for College

The cost of higher education continues to rise, making early planning essential. Understanding your savings options can help you build a meaningful college fund without derailing your other financial goals.

## How Much Does College Cost?

As of recent data, average annual costs (tuition, fees, room, and board) are approximately:

- **Public in-state university:** $22,000–$28,000 per year
- **Public out-of-state university:** $38,000–$45,000 per year
- **Private university:** $50,000–$60,000 per year

Over four years, a public in-state degree can cost $90,000 or more, and costs tend to rise 3–5% annually.

## 529 Plans

A 529 plan is the most popular college savings vehicle. Key features include:

- **Tax-free growth:** Earnings grow federal tax-free when used for qualified education expenses
- **State tax benefits:** Many states offer a deduction or credit for contributions to their own plan
- **High contribution limits:** Lifetime limits often exceed $300,000 per beneficiary
- **Flexible use:** Funds cover tuition, room and board, books, computers, and K-12 tuition up to $10,000 per year
- **Transferable:** You can change the beneficiary to another family member if the original beneficiary does not need the funds

## Coverdell Education Savings Accounts

A Coverdell ESA is another tax-advantaged option with some differences:

- Annual contribution limit of $2,000 per beneficiary
- Income limits apply (modified AGI under $110,000 for single filers, $220,000 for joint)
- Funds can be used for K-12 and college expenses
- More investment flexibility than most 529 plans

Because of the low contribution limit, a Coverdell ESA works best as a supplement to a 529 plan.

## The Power of Starting Early

If you invest $200 per month starting at a child's birth and earn an average 7% annual return, you would have roughly $86,000 by the time they turn 18. Waiting until the child is 8 years old and making the same monthly contribution yields only about $38,000.

## Financial Aid Considerations

Saving for college does not automatically disqualify your child from financial aid:

- **529 plans owned by a parent** are assessed at a maximum rate of 5.64% of the account value in the federal aid formula
- **Student-owned assets** are assessed at 20%, so keeping accounts in the parent's name is generally more favorable
- **Grandparent-owned 529 plans** no longer count as student income on the FAFSA under recent rule changes

Do not sacrifice your own retirement savings to fund college. Students can borrow for education, but you cannot borrow for retirement.
