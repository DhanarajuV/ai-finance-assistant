# Bull and Bear Markets

The terms "bull market" and "bear market" describe the general direction and sentiment of the stock market. Understanding these cycles helps you stay calm, make informed decisions, and avoid costly emotional mistakes during periods of market turbulence.

## Definitions

### Bull Market
A bull market is a period of **rising stock prices**, generally defined as a sustained increase of **20% or more** from a recent low. Bull markets are characterized by:

- Widespread investor optimism and confidence
- Strong economic growth and low unemployment
- Rising corporate earnings
- Increased buying activity

### Bear Market
A bear market is a period of **falling stock prices**, generally defined as a decline of **20% or more** from a recent high. Bear markets are characterized by:

- Widespread pessimism and fear
- Slowing economic growth or recession
- Declining corporate earnings
- Increased selling activity

A decline of 10–20% is typically called a **correction**, which is milder and more frequent than a full bear market.

## Historical Examples

### Notable Bull Markets
- **1982–2000:** One of the longest bull markets in history, fueled by technological innovation and economic expansion. The S&P 500 gained over 1,400%.
- **2009–2020:** Following the Great Recession, this 11-year bull run was the longest on record, driven by low interest rates and strong corporate earnings.
- **2020–2021:** A rapid recovery from the COVID-19 crash, with the S&P 500 doubling from its March 2020 low.

### Notable Bear Markets
- **2000–2002 (Dot-Com Bust):** The S&P 500 fell about 49% as the technology bubble burst.
- **2007–2009 (Great Recession):** The S&P 500 dropped approximately 57%, the worst decline since the Great Depression.
- **2020 (COVID-19 Crash):** The S&P 500 fell 34% in just over a month, though it recovered remarkably quickly.

## Key Patterns to Understand

- **Bull markets last longer than bear markets.** The average bull market lasts about 4–5 years; the average bear market lasts about 9–16 months.
- **Bear markets are a normal part of investing.** They have occurred roughly every 3–5 years on average.
- **Recovery always follows decline.** Every bear market in U.S. history has eventually been followed by a new bull market and new all-time highs.

## How to Behave in Each Market

### During a Bull Market
- **Stay disciplined** – do not let euphoria push you into overly risky investments
- **Stick to your plan** – continue your regular investment strategy
- **Rebalance** – if stocks have grown beyond your target allocation, trim back to your plan
- **Avoid chasing performance** – just because something has gone up does not mean it will continue

### During a Bear Market
- **Do not panic sell** – selling during a downturn locks in your losses
- **Keep investing** – if you are dollar-cost averaging, downturns mean you are buying shares at lower prices
- **Review, do not react** – check that your allocation still matches your goals, but avoid making emotional changes
- **Remember history** – every past bear market has been temporary

## The Cost of Panic Selling

If you invested $10,000 in the S&P 500 at the start of 2008 and held through the crash and recovery, your investment would have grown substantially by 2023. If you sold at the bottom in March 2009 and moved to cash, you would have locked in a roughly 50% loss and missed one of the greatest bull markets in history.

Staying invested through the discomfort is one of the most important skills in building long-term wealth.

## Key Takeaways

- Bull markets rise 20%+ from lows; bear markets fall 20%+ from highs
- Bear markets are shorter and less frequent than bull markets
- Every bear market in history has been followed by recovery
- Do not panic sell during downturns; stay the course
- Discipline during both euphoria and fear is what separates successful investors
