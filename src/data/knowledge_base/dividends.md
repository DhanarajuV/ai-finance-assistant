# Understanding Dividends

A dividend is a payment made by a company to its shareholders, typically from its profits. When you own stock in a company that pays dividends, you receive a portion of the company's earnings simply for holding the shares. Dividends provide a way to earn income from your investments without selling them.

## How Dividends Work

Companies that generate consistent profits may choose to distribute a portion of those earnings to shareholders. Dividends are usually paid quarterly (four times per year), though some companies pay monthly or annually.

When a dividend is declared, three key dates matter:

- **Declaration date:** The company announces the dividend amount and payment schedule
- **Ex-dividend date:** You must own the stock before this date to receive the upcoming dividend. If you buy on or after this date, you will not receive the next payment.
- **Payment date:** The date the dividend is actually deposited into your account

## Dividend Yield

The **dividend yield** tells you how much income a stock pays relative to its price. It is calculated as:

**Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Stock Price**

For example, if a stock costs $100 and pays $3 per year in dividends, its yield is 3%. A higher yield means more income per dollar invested, but extremely high yields (above 6–8%) can be a warning sign that the company may not sustain the payment.

## DRIP: Dividend Reinvestment Plans

A **DRIP** automatically uses your dividend payments to purchase additional shares of the same stock or fund. Instead of receiving cash, you accumulate more shares, which then generate their own dividends.

Benefits of DRIPs:
- Harnesses the power of compound growth
- Purchases happen automatically with no effort
- Many brokerages offer fractional share reinvestment
- No transaction fees in most cases

Over decades, reinvested dividends can account for a significant portion of your total investment returns. Historically, reinvested dividends have contributed roughly 40% of the S&P 500's total return.

## Dividend Aristocrats

**Dividend Aristocrats** are S&P 500 companies that have increased their dividend every year for at least 25 consecutive years. Examples include:

- **Johnson & Johnson** – healthcare giant
- **Coca-Cola** – beverage company
- **Procter & Gamble** – consumer goods
- **3M** – diversified manufacturer

These companies are valued for their reliability and financial discipline. A long track record of dividend increases suggests stable earnings and shareholder-friendly management.

## Qualified vs. Ordinary Dividends

Not all dividends are taxed the same way:

### Qualified Dividends
- Taxed at the lower long-term capital gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income)
- Must meet holding period requirements (held the stock for more than 60 days)
- Must be paid by a U.S. corporation or qualifying foreign corporation

### Ordinary (Non-Qualified) Dividends
- Taxed at your regular income tax rate, which can be significantly higher
- Includes dividends from REITs, money market funds, and some foreign stocks

Understanding this distinction can help you make tax-efficient investment decisions, especially in taxable brokerage accounts.

## Key Takeaways

- Dividends are cash payments from companies to shareholders
- Dividend yield measures income relative to stock price
- You must own shares before the ex-dividend date to receive payment
- DRIPs reinvest dividends automatically, boosting compound growth
- Dividend Aristocrats have raised dividends for 25+ consecutive years
- Qualified dividends receive favorable tax treatment over ordinary dividends
