# Budgeting Basics

A budget is simply a plan for how you spend your money. Without one, it is easy to overspend in some areas and neglect important goals like saving and investing. The good news is that budgeting does not have to be complicated.

## The 50/30/20 Rule

One of the most popular budgeting frameworks divides your after-tax income into three buckets:

- **50% Needs** — Housing, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments, and transportation.
- **30% Wants** — Dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, hobbies, and non-essential shopping.
- **20% Savings & Debt Repayment** — Emergency fund contributions, retirement accounts, extra debt payments, and other financial goals.

This rule is a starting point, not a rigid law. If you live in a high-cost area, your needs percentage may be higher, and you will need to adjust the other categories accordingly.

## Pay Yourself First

Instead of saving whatever is left at the end of the month, set up automatic transfers to your savings or investment accounts on payday. This flips the typical approach:

1. Income arrives.
2. Savings are automatically moved out.
3. You spend what remains.

By treating savings like a non-negotiable bill, you build wealth consistently without relying on willpower.

## Zero-Based Budgeting

In a zero-based budget, every dollar of income is assigned a specific job so that income minus planned spending equals zero. Categories might include rent, groceries, gas, entertainment, savings, and giving. At the end of the month, no money is left "unassigned."

This method works well for people who want maximum control over their finances. It does require more effort to maintain, but it eliminates the mystery of where your money went.

## Tracking Your Expenses

You cannot manage what you do not measure. Start by tracking every purchase for at least 30 days. You can use:

- **A simple spreadsheet** — Free and fully customizable.
- **Pen and paper** — Low-tech but effective for building awareness.
- **Budgeting apps** — Tools like Mint, YNAB (You Need A Budget), and EveryDollar connect to your bank accounts and categorize transactions automatically.

After a month of tracking, patterns emerge. You may discover that small daily purchases add up to hundreds of dollars, or that a subscription you forgot about is still charging you.

## Tips for Sticking to a Budget

- **Review weekly.** A quick five-minute check keeps you on track before small overspends snowball.
- **Use cash envelopes for problem categories.** When the cash is gone, spending in that category stops.
- **Build in fun money.** A budget that is too restrictive will not last. Allow yourself guilt-free spending within limits.
- **Adjust as life changes.** A budget is a living document. Update it when your income, expenses, or goals change.

## Why Budgeting Matters

Budgeting is the foundation of every other financial goal. Whether you want to pay off debt, build an emergency fund, or start investing, it all begins with knowing where your money goes and directing it intentionally.
