# Understanding Net Worth

Net worth is the single best snapshot of your overall financial health. It tells you what you own minus what you owe, giving you one number that summarizes where you stand.

## How to Calculate Net Worth

The formula is straightforward:

**Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities**

### Assets (What You Own)

- Cash in checking and savings accounts
- Investment accounts (brokerage, retirement, 401(k), IRA)
- Real estate (current market value of your home)
- Vehicles (current resale value)
- Other valuables (jewelry, collectibles, business equity)

### Liabilities (What You Owe)

- Mortgage balance
- Student loans
- Auto loans
- Credit card balances
- Personal loans
- Medical debt

**Example:** If you have $150,000 in assets and $80,000 in liabilities, your net worth is $70,000.

## Why Tracking Net Worth Matters

- **It reveals the full picture.** A high income means little if debt is growing faster than savings. Net worth captures both sides.
- **It measures progress over time.** Checking your net worth quarterly or annually shows whether your financial decisions are moving you forward.
- **It motivates good behavior.** Watching your net worth climb reinforces saving and investing habits. Seeing it stall can prompt useful course corrections.
- **It shifts focus from income to wealth.** Building wealth is about the gap between what you earn and what you spend, accumulated over time.

## Typical Net Worth Benchmarks by Age

These rough benchmarks can help you gauge where you stand relative to peers. They assume steady saving and investing over a career:

| Age | Suggested Net Worth Target |
|-----|---------------------------|
| 25 | $0 to $25,000 |
| 30 | Roughly half your annual salary |
| 35 | 1x your annual salary |
| 40 | 2x your annual salary |
| 50 | 4x your annual salary |
| 60 | 6-8x your annual salary |

These are general guidelines, not rigid rules. Someone who started saving later or lives in a high-cost area may be on a different timeline, and that is perfectly fine.

## Tips for Growing Your Net Worth

- **Increase the gap.** Earn more, spend less, or both. The wider the gap between income and expenses, the faster net worth grows.
- **Pay down high-interest debt first.** Credit card debt at 20% interest erodes net worth faster than most investments can build it.
- **Invest consistently.** Money sitting in a savings account grows slowly. Investing in diversified assets helps your wealth compound over decades.
- **Avoid lifestyle inflation.** When your income rises, resist the urge to increase spending by the same amount. Direct raises toward savings and investments.
- **Track it regularly.** Use a spreadsheet or a free tool to update your net worth at least once a quarter.

## A Negative Net Worth Is Not the End

Many young adults start with a negative net worth due to student loans. This is common and temporary as long as you are making payments and building assets. The important thing is the direction of the trend, not the starting point.
