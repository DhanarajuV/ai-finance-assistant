# A Brief History of the Stock Market

Understanding the history of stock markets helps put today's headlines in perspective. Markets have survived wars, pandemics, financial crises, and technological disruptions — and have trended upward over the long run.

## How It All Started

The concept of trading shares dates back to the 1600s, when the Dutch East India Company issued stock to fund voyages. The Amsterdam Stock Exchange, established in 1602, is widely considered the world's first formal stock exchange.

In the United States, the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) was founded in 1792 when 24 brokers signed the Buttonwood Agreement under a tree on Wall Street. The exchange grew alongside the American economy, becoming the largest in the world.

## Major Crashes and Recoveries

### The Great Crash of 1929

In October 1929, the stock market lost roughly 25% of its value in two days. The crash marked the beginning of the Great Depression, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average did not fully recover to its 1929 peak until 1954. This event led to major reforms, including the creation of the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) in 1934.

### The 2008 Financial Crisis

Fueled by risky mortgage lending and complex financial products, the housing bubble burst in 2007-2008. The S&P 500 fell approximately 57% from its October 2007 peak to its March 2009 low. Banks failed, unemployment surged, and governments intervened with massive bailouts. Yet by 2013, the S&P 500 had recovered to new highs.

### The 2020 COVID-19 Crash

When the pandemic shut down the global economy in early 2020, the S&P 500 dropped about 34% in just over a month — one of the fastest declines in history. Remarkably, the market recovered to pre-crash levels within about five months, driven by government stimulus, low interest rates, and a rapid shift to digital services.

## The Long-Term Upward Trend

Despite these dramatic downturns, the overall trajectory of the stock market has been upward:

- The S&P 500 has delivered an average annual return of roughly 10% (before inflation) since its inception.
- A hypothetical $1,000 invested in the S&P 500 in 1980 would have grown to over $100,000 by 2024, with dividends reinvested.
- Every major crash in U.S. history has eventually been followed by a recovery to new all-time highs.

## Lessons from Market History

- **Crashes are normal.** Market declines of 10% or more happen roughly once a year on average. Declines of 20% or more occur every few years.
- **Time in the market beats timing the market.** Investors who stayed invested through downturns have historically been rewarded.
- **Diversification helps.** Spreading investments across asset classes and geographies reduces the impact of any single downturn.
- **Regulations evolve.** Each crisis has led to new rules designed to prevent a repeat, though new risks always emerge.
- **Patience is an edge.** The stock market rewards long-term thinking. Short-term volatility is the price of admission for long-term growth.
