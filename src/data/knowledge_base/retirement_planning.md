# Retirement Planning Fundamentals

Retirement planning is one of the most important financial goals you will pursue. The earlier you start, the more time your money has to grow through the power of compounding.

## When to Start

The best time to start saving for retirement is as early as possible. A 25-year-old who invests $300 per month at a 7% average annual return will have roughly $720,000 by age 65. Waiting until age 35 to start the same contributions yields only about $340,000. Those ten years of delay cut the final balance by more than half.

## How Much to Save: The 15% Rule

A widely used guideline is to save at least **15% of your gross income** for retirement. This includes any employer match you receive. For example:

- If you earn $60,000 per year, aim to save $9,000 annually
- If your employer matches 3%, you contribute 12% and they add 3%
- If 15% feels out of reach today, start with what you can and increase by 1% each year

## Understanding Social Security

Social Security provides a foundation of retirement income, but it was never designed to be your only source. Key points:

- Benefits are based on your highest 35 years of earnings
- You can claim as early as age 62, but your benefit will be permanently reduced
- Full retirement age is 67 for most people born after 1960
- Delaying until age 70 increases your monthly benefit by about 8% per year past full retirement age

## Retirement Calculators

Online retirement calculators help you estimate whether you are on track. When using one, you will typically enter:

- Your current age and target retirement age
- Current savings and monthly contributions
- Expected rate of return and inflation rate
- Desired annual spending in retirement

Run the numbers with different assumptions. Markets do not deliver the same return every year, so testing optimistic and conservative scenarios gives you a more realistic range.

## The 4% Withdrawal Rule

The 4% rule is a guideline for how much you can withdraw from your portfolio each year in retirement without running out of money over a 30-year period. Here is how it works:

- **Year one:** Withdraw 4% of your total portfolio. If you have $1,000,000, you withdraw $40,000.
- **Subsequent years:** Adjust the prior year's withdrawal for inflation.

This rule originated from a 1994 study by financial planner William Bengen. It is not a guarantee, but it provides a useful starting point. Some planners now suggest 3.5% for added safety, especially given longer life expectancies.

## Putting It All Together

A solid retirement plan combines consistent saving, smart investing, and realistic expectations. Start early, aim for 15%, understand what Social Security will and will not provide, and use the 4% rule to estimate how large your nest egg needs to be.
