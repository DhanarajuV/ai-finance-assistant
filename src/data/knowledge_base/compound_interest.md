# The Power of Compound Interest

Compound interest is often called the eighth wonder of the world. It is the process where the interest you earn itself earns interest, creating a snowball effect that accelerates your wealth over time.

## Simple vs. Compound Interest

**Simple interest** is calculated only on your original principal. If you invest $1,000 at 5% simple interest, you earn $50 every year regardless of how long you invest.

**Compound interest** is calculated on your principal plus all previously earned interest:

| Year | Simple Interest | Compound Interest |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1 | $1,050 | $1,050 |
| 5 | $1,250 | $1,276 |
| 10 | $1,500 | $1,629 |
| 20 | $2,000 | $2,653 |
| 30 | $2,500 | $4,322 |

After 30 years, compound interest produces 73% more than simple interest on the same investment.

## How Compounding Works

The formula is: **A = P x (1 + r/n)^(n x t)**

Where:
- **A** = final amount
- **P** = principal (starting amount)
- **r** = annual interest rate (as a decimal)
- **n** = number of times interest compounds per year
- **t** = number of years

The more frequently interest compounds, the faster your money grows, though the difference between monthly and daily compounding is relatively small.

## The Rule of 72

The **Rule of 72** is a quick way to estimate how long it takes for your money to double. Divide 72 by your annual rate of return:

- At 6% return: 72 / 6 = **12 years** to double
- At 8% return: 72 / 8 = **9 years** to double
- At 10% return: 72 / 10 = **7.2 years** to double

At a 10% return, $10,000 becomes roughly $20,000 in about 7 years, $40,000 in 14 years, and $80,000 in 21 years without adding a single dollar.

## Why Starting Early Matters

Consider two investors:

**Early Emma** starts investing $200/month at age 25 and stops at age 35 (10 years, $24,000 total invested).

**Late Larry** starts investing $200/month at age 35 and continues until age 65 (30 years, $72,000 total invested).

Assuming a 7% annual return:
- Emma at age 65: **~$245,000**
- Larry at age 65: **~$228,000**

Emma invested one-third the money but ended up with more because her money had an extra decade to compound. This is the most compelling reason to start investing as early as possible.

## Key Takeaways

- Compound interest earns returns on your returns, creating exponential growth
- Time is your greatest asset; even small amounts grow significantly over decades
- The Rule of 72 helps you quickly estimate doubling time
- Starting early matters more than investing large amounts later
- Reinvesting dividends and gains keeps the compounding engine running
