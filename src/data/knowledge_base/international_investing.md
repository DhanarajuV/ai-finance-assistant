# International Investing

Investing beyond your home country's borders can improve diversification and expose your portfolio to growth opportunities around the world.

## Why Invest Globally?

The United States represents roughly 60% of global stock market capitalization. That means about 40% of investable opportunities exist outside U.S. borders:

- **Diversification:** Different economies grow at different rates and respond differently to global events
- **Access to growth:** Some of the fastest-growing economies and companies are located outside the U.S.
- **Reduced concentration risk:** Relying entirely on one country's market ties your wealth to that single economy

## Developed vs. Emerging Markets

### Developed Markets

Countries with established, stable economies and mature financial systems:

- United Kingdom, Germany, France, Japan, Australia, Canada
- Tend to behave somewhat similarly to U.S. stocks but still provide meaningful diversification

### Emerging Markets

Countries with rapidly growing economies that may carry higher risk and higher potential reward:

- China, India, Brazil, Taiwan, South Korea
- Greater volatility due to political instability, less regulatory oversight, and currency fluctuations
- Exposure to demographic trends and economic growth that developed markets may lack

## Currency Risk

When you invest in foreign stocks, your returns are affected by changes in exchange rates:

- If you buy a European stock and the euro weakens against the dollar, your returns in dollar terms decrease even if the stock price rose in euros
- The reverse is also true — a strengthening foreign currency boosts your dollar-denominated returns
- Over long periods, currency effects tend to even out, but they add short-term volatility

Some funds offer currency-hedged versions that reduce this risk, though hedging adds a small cost.

## International ETFs

ETFs make global investing simple and affordable:

- **Total international stock ETFs** (e.g., VXUS, IXUS) — broad exposure to developed and emerging markets
- **Developed market ETFs** (e.g., VEA, IEFA) — focused on established economies outside the U.S.
- **Emerging market ETFs** (e.g., VWO, IEMG) — focused on higher-growth developing economies
- **Region-specific ETFs** — target a single country or region like Europe or Asia-Pacific

A single total international ETF is often sufficient for most investors seeking broad global diversification.

## Home Bias

Home bias is the tendency to invest disproportionately in your own country's stock market. While familiarity feels comfortable, it concentrates risk. Many financial professionals suggest allocating **20% to 40%** of your stock portfolio to international holdings, though the right amount depends on your goals and risk tolerance.

A low-cost total international ETF is a straightforward way to add global diversification to your portfolio.
