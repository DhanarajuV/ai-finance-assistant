# Understanding Mutual Funds

A mutual fund is an investment vehicle that pools money from many investors to buy a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities. A professional fund manager makes the buying and selling decisions on behalf of all the investors in the fund.

## How Mutual Funds Work

When you invest in a mutual fund, you purchase **shares** of the fund. Each share represents a proportional ownership of the fund's entire portfolio. The price of one share is called the **Net Asset Value (NAV)**, which is calculated at the end of each trading day by dividing the total value of all the fund's assets by the number of outstanding shares.

- You send money to the fund company
- The fund manager invests that money according to the fund's stated strategy
- You own a slice of the entire portfolio
- The NAV updates once per day after the market closes

## Types of Mutual Funds

### Index Funds
These passively track a market index like the S&P 500. They aim to match the market's performance rather than beat it. They typically have very low fees.

### Actively Managed Funds
A fund manager actively picks investments trying to outperform the market. These carry higher fees, and research shows most fail to beat their benchmark index over the long term.

### Other Common Types
- **Bond funds** – invest in government or corporate bonds
- **Balanced funds** – mix of stocks and bonds
- **Money market funds** – invest in short-term, low-risk debt
- **Sector funds** – focus on a specific industry like technology or healthcare

## Expense Ratios

The **expense ratio** is the annual fee expressed as a percentage of your investment. It covers management, administration, and operating costs.

- Index funds: typically 0.03%–0.20%
- Actively managed funds: typically 0.50%–1.50%

A 1% difference in fees may sound small, but over decades it can cost you tens of thousands of dollars in lost growth.

## Minimum Investments

Many mutual funds require a minimum initial investment, often ranging from $500 to $3,000. Some funds designed for retirement accounts may have lower minimums or none at all.

## Mutual Funds vs. ETFs

| Feature | Mutual Funds | ETFs |
|---|---|---|
| Trading | Once per day at NAV | Throughout the day like stocks |
| Minimum investment | Often $500–$3,000 | Price of one share |
| Expense ratios | Varies widely | Generally lower |
| Tax efficiency | Less tax-efficient | More tax-efficient |
| Automatic investing | Easy to set up | Harder to automate |

## Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Instant diversification
- Professional management
- Easy automatic investing and reinvestment
- Accessible for beginners

**Cons:**
- Fees can eat into returns, especially with actively managed funds
- You can only buy or sell at the end-of-day NAV
- Potential capital gains tax distributions even if you did not sell
- Minimum investment requirements can be a barrier

Mutual funds remain one of the most popular ways for everyday investors to build wealth over time, especially through employer-sponsored retirement plans like 401(k)s.
