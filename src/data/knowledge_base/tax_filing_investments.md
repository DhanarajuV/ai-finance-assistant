# Tax Filing for Investors

Investing creates tax obligations beyond a standard W-2 filing. Understanding the key forms and reporting requirements helps you file accurately and avoid surprises at tax time.

## Form 1099-B: Proceeds from Broker Transactions

Your brokerage sends a 1099-B for each year you sell investments. It reports:

- The date you bought and sold each security
- Your proceeds (sale price) and cost basis (purchase price)
- Whether the gain or loss is short-term (held one year or less) or long-term (held more than one year)

This information flows onto **Schedule D** and **Form 8949** of your tax return. Most tax software imports 1099-B data directly from your brokerage, but always verify the numbers.

## Form 1099-DIV: Dividends and Distributions

If you received dividends or capital gains distributions from mutual funds or ETFs, you will get a 1099-DIV:

- **Box 1a — Ordinary dividends:** Taxed at your regular income tax rate
- **Box 1b — Qualified dividends:** Taxed at the lower long-term capital gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income)
- **Box 2a — Capital gains distributions:** Long-term gains passed through from a fund, taxed at long-term rates

You owe tax on these distributions even if you reinvested them rather than taking cash.

## Reporting Capital Gains

Capital gains are categorized by holding period:

- **Short-term gains** (held one year or less) are taxed as ordinary income
- **Long-term gains** (held more than one year) receive preferential tax rates

You can offset gains with losses. If your losses exceed your gains, you can deduct up to **$3,000** of net losses against ordinary income per year. Remaining losses carry forward to future tax years.

## Wash Sale Rule

The IRS wash sale rule prevents you from claiming a tax loss if you buy a "substantially identical" security within **30 days before or after** the sale:

- You sell Fund A at a loss on March 15
- You buy Fund A again on April 1 (within 30 days)
- The loss is disallowed and added to the cost basis of the new shares

To avoid wash sales, wait at least 31 days before repurchasing, or buy a similar but not identical fund.

## Estimated Tax Payments

If you have significant investment income not subject to withholding, you may need to make quarterly estimated tax payments. This applies if you expect to owe **$1,000 or more** beyond what is withheld from wages.

Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15 of the following year. Use **IRS Form 1040-ES** to calculate and submit payments.

## Tips for Tax Time

- Download 1099 forms from your brokerage as soon as they are available
- Verify cost basis, especially for shares transferred between brokerages
- Track wash sales across all accounts, including IRAs
- Consider a tax professional if you have complex transactions
