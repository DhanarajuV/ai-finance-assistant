# Financial Glossary (N-Z)

A continuation of our financial glossary covering terms from N through Z. Use this alongside the A-M glossary as a quick reference.

---

## NAV (Net Asset Value)

NAV is the per-share value of a mutual fund or ETF, calculated by dividing the total value of all the fund's assets minus liabilities by the number of outstanding shares. Mutual funds are priced at their NAV once per day after the market closes. ETFs trade throughout the day at market prices that may differ slightly from NAV.

## P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings Ratio)

The P/E ratio is a stock's current price divided by its earnings per share. It tells you how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar of earnings. A P/E of 20 means investors pay $20 for every $1 of annual earnings. Higher P/E ratios may indicate growth expectations; lower P/E ratios may suggest a stock is undervalued or that growth is slowing.

## Portfolio

A portfolio is the collection of all your investments — stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs, real estate, cash, and any other assets. Portfolio management involves choosing the right mix of assets (asset allocation) and adjusting it over time to align with your goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon.

## Quantitative Easing (QE)

Quantitative easing is a monetary policy tool used by central banks (like the Federal Reserve) to stimulate the economy. The central bank purchases large quantities of government bonds or other securities to inject money into the financial system, lower interest rates, and encourage lending and investment. QE was used extensively after the 2008 financial crisis and during the 2020 pandemic.

## Recession

A recession is a significant decline in economic activity lasting more than a few months. It is commonly defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth, though the official determination in the U.S. is made by the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER). Recessions typically involve rising unemployment, falling consumer spending, and declining business profits.

## SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission)

The SEC is the U.S. federal agency responsible for regulating the securities industry, enforcing securities laws, and protecting investors. It oversees stock exchanges, broker-dealers, investment advisors, and mutual funds. The SEC was created in 1934 in response to the stock market crash of 1929.

## Treasury Securities

Treasury securities are debt instruments issued by the U.S. government to fund its operations. They are considered among the safest investments in the world because they are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government. Types include Treasury bills (mature in one year or less), Treasury notes (2-10 years), and Treasury bonds (20-30 years).

## Underwriting

Underwriting is the process by which a financial institution (typically an investment bank) evaluates and assumes the risk of issuing new securities. In an IPO, the underwriter helps set the offering price, buys the shares from the company, and sells them to investors. Underwriting also applies to insurance and lending, where it refers to assessing and pricing risk.

## Volatility

Volatility measures how much and how quickly the price of an investment changes over time. High volatility means large price swings (up or down); low volatility means more stable prices. Volatility is often measured by standard deviation or by the VIX index (sometimes called the "fear index"), which tracks expected volatility in the S&P 500.

## Wall Street

Wall Street is a street in lower Manhattan, New York City, home to the New York Stock Exchange and many major financial institutions. The term is widely used as shorthand for the U.S. financial industry and markets as a whole. When someone says "Wall Street is up today," they generally mean the stock market rose.

## Yield

Yield is the income return on an investment, expressed as a percentage. For stocks, yield usually refers to the dividend yield. For bonds, yield refers to the annual interest payment relative to the bond's price. Yield is a key metric for income-focused investors comparing different investment options.

## Zero-Coupon Bond

A zero-coupon bond is a bond that does not make periodic interest payments. Instead, it is sold at a deep discount to its face value and pays the full face value at maturity. For example, you might buy a zero-coupon bond for $600 that pays $1,000 in 10 years. The difference between the purchase price and the face value represents your return. These bonds are sensitive to interest rate changes.
