# Common Investment Mistakes

Every investor makes mistakes, but learning about the most common ones can help you avoid costly errors. Most of these pitfalls are driven by emotion, lack of planning, or misunderstanding how markets work.

## 1. Trying to Time the Market

Timing the market means attempting to buy at the bottom and sell at the top. Research has shown that missing just the 10 best trading days over a 20-year period can cut your total return by more than half. The best days often occur very close to the worst days, during periods of high volatility.

**Better approach:** Invest consistently through dollar-cost averaging regardless of market conditions.

## 2. Not Diversifying

Putting all your money into a single stock, sector, or asset class exposes you to unnecessary risk. If that one investment declines sharply, your entire portfolio suffers.

**Better approach:** Spread investments across multiple asset classes, geographies, and sectors. A simple target-date fund or a three-fund portfolio can provide instant diversification.

## 3. Panic Selling

When markets drop, fear takes over. Selling during a downturn locks in losses and removes any chance of participating in the recovery. Historically, investors who sold during crashes earned significantly less than those who stayed invested.

**Better approach:** Build a portfolio you can hold through downturns. If a 30% decline would cause you to sell everything, your allocation is too aggressive for your risk tolerance.

## 4. Chasing Performance

It is tempting to pile into whatever stock or fund had the best returns last year. However, past performance is one of the least reliable predictors of future results. Last year's top performer is often next year's laggard.

**Better approach:** Choose investments based on your goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance — not recent returns.

## 5. Ignoring Fees

Investment fees may seem small as percentages, but they compound dramatically over time. A 1% annual fee on a $100,000 portfolio can cost over $175,000 in lost growth over 30 years compared to a 0.05% fee.

**Better approach:** Compare expense ratios before investing. Favor low-cost index funds and ETFs. Check for hidden fees like sales loads, 12b-1 fees, and trading commissions.

## 6. Not Starting Early Enough

The most powerful force in investing is compound growth, and it needs time to work. An investor who starts at age 25 and invests $300 per month at a 7% average return will have roughly $720,000 by age 65. Waiting until age 35 yields only about $340,000 — less than half.

**Better approach:** Start investing as soon as possible, even if the amounts are small. Time matters more than the size of your initial investment.

## Key Takeaways

- Have a written investment plan and stick to it.
- Automate your contributions to remove emotion from the process.
- Keep costs low and diversification broad.
- Focus on time in the market, not timing the market.
- Review your portfolio periodically, but resist the urge to react to every headline.
