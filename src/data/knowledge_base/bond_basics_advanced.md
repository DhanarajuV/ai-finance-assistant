# Bond Basics — Beyond the Fundamentals

Once you understand that a bond is essentially a loan you make to a government or corporation in exchange for regular interest payments, the next step is learning how bond prices move, what risks to watch for, and how to use bonds strategically.

## Bond Prices and Yields: The Inverse Relationship

This is the single most important concept in bond investing: **when interest rates rise, bond prices fall, and when interest rates fall, bond prices rise.**

Imagine you own a bond paying 3% interest. If new bonds are issued at 5%, no one will pay full price for your 3% bond. Its market price drops until the effective yield matches current rates. The reverse happens when rates fall — your higher-paying bond becomes more valuable.

This only matters if you sell before maturity. If you hold a bond to maturity, you receive the full face value regardless of price fluctuations.

## Duration: Measuring Interest Rate Sensitivity

Duration measures how sensitive a bond's price is to changes in interest rates, expressed in years:

- A bond with a duration of 5 years will lose approximately 5% in value if interest rates rise by 1%
- Longer-duration bonds are more sensitive to rate changes
- Shorter-duration bonds are more stable but typically offer lower yields

When you expect interest rates to rise, shorter-duration bonds reduce your exposure to price declines.

## Credit Risk

Credit risk is the chance that the bond issuer fails to make interest payments or repay the principal. Bonds are rated by agencies like Moody's and S&P:

- **AAA to BBB (investment grade):** Lower risk, lower yield. U.S. Treasury bonds are considered the safest.
- **BB and below (high yield or "junk"):** Higher risk, higher yield. Greater chance of default.

Diversifying across many issuers through a bond fund reduces the impact of any single default.

## The Yield Curve

The yield curve plots interest rates of bonds with different maturities, typically from 3 months to 30 years. In a normal environment:

- Short-term bonds offer lower yields
- Long-term bonds offer higher yields to compensate for the added risk of tying up money longer

When the yield curve **inverts** — short-term rates exceed long-term rates — it has historically been a signal that a recession may be approaching.

## Bond Laddering

A bond ladder is a strategy where you buy bonds with staggered maturity dates:

- Buy bonds maturing in 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 years
- As each bond matures, reinvest the proceeds into a new 5-year bond
- This creates a rolling cycle of maturities

Benefits of laddering:

- **Reduces interest rate risk** by spreading purchases across different rate environments
- **Provides regular liquidity** as bonds mature at intervals
- **Smooths out returns** compared to buying all bonds at a single maturity

Understanding duration, credit ratings, and strategies like laddering gives you a practical framework for building a bond allocation that matches your needs.
