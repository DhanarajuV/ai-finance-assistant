# Dollar-Cost Averaging

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is an investment strategy where you invest a fixed amount of money at regular intervals, regardless of what the market is doing. Instead of trying to time the perfect moment to invest, you invest consistently and let the math work in your favor.

## How Dollar-Cost Averaging Works

With DCA, you commit to investing the same dollar amount on a set schedule. When prices are high, your fixed amount buys fewer shares. When prices are low, the same amount buys more shares. Over time, this averages out your cost per share.

If you have ever contributed to a 401(k) through payroll deductions, you are already practicing dollar-cost averaging.

## Example: Monthly Investing

Suppose you invest $500 per month into a fund over five months:

| Month | Share Price | Shares Purchased |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| January | $50 | 10.0 |
| February | $40 | 12.5 |
| March | $25 | 20.0 |
| April | $30 | 16.7 |
| May | $45 | 11.1 |

**Total invested:** $2,500
**Total shares:** 70.3
**Average cost per share:** $35.56

If you had invested the entire $2,500 in January at $50 per share, you would own only 50 shares. By spreading your purchases, you ended up with 70.3 shares at a lower average cost.

## Why DCA Reduces Timing Risk

Nobody can consistently predict market direction. DCA removes that pressure by:

- **Eliminating the need to time the market** – you invest on schedule, not on emotion
- **Reducing the impact of volatility** – buying more shares when prices drop lowers your average cost
- **Building discipline** – a regular schedule keeps you investing through good and bad markets
- **Lowering regret risk** – you avoid the pain of investing a large sum right before a downturn

## Lump Sum vs. DCA

Research shows that **lump sum investing** outperforms DCA roughly two-thirds of the time because markets tend to rise over time, so getting money invested sooner captures more growth.

However, DCA has important psychological advantages:

- Less stressful than committing everything at once
- Protects against bad timing if the market drops immediately after investing
- Better than not investing at all if fear keeps you on the sidelines

If you receive a large sum, lump sum investing has a statistical edge. If you earn a regular paycheck, DCA is the natural and practical choice.

## Practical Tips

- **Automate your contributions** so you invest without thinking about it
- **Stay consistent** even when the market feels scary
- **Choose low-cost index funds** to pair DCA with broad diversification
- **Increase your amount over time** as your income grows

The greatest benefit of DCA is that it turns investing into a habit rather than a decision.
