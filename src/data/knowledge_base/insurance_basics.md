# Insurance Basics for Financial Planning

Insurance is a cornerstone of a sound financial plan. While it is not as exciting as investing, having the right coverage protects you from catastrophic financial losses that could wipe out years of savings and progress.

## Why Insurance Matters

Insurance transfers risk from you to an insurance company in exchange for a premium. Without it, a single unexpected event — a serious illness, a car accident, or a premature death — could devastate your finances.

Think of insurance as a financial safety net. You hope you never need it, but it is essential to have before something goes wrong.

## Types of Insurance

### Health Insurance
- Covers medical expenses including doctor visits, hospital stays, prescriptions, and preventive care.
- Without health insurance, a single hospital stay can cost tens of thousands of dollars.
- Options include employer-sponsored plans, marketplace plans (Healthcare.gov), and government programs like Medicaid and Medicare.
- **Key terms:** Premium (monthly cost), deductible (what you pay before insurance kicks in), copay (fixed cost per visit), and out-of-pocket maximum (the most you pay in a year).

### Life Insurance
Life insurance pays a benefit to your beneficiaries if you die. It is most important if others depend on your income.

**Term Life Insurance:**
- Covers you for a specific period (10, 20, or 30 years).
- Much more affordable than whole life.
- Pays out only if you die during the term.
- Best for most people — covers you during your highest-need years (raising children, paying a mortgage).

**Whole Life Insurance:**
- Covers you for your entire life as long as premiums are paid.
- Includes a cash value component that grows over time.
- Significantly more expensive than term life (often 5-15 times more).
- The investment component typically earns low returns compared to investing on your own.

For most people, **term life insurance is the better choice.** It provides the coverage you need at a fraction of the cost. You can invest the savings from lower premiums on your own.

### Disability Insurance
- Replaces a portion of your income (typically 60-70%) if you become unable to work due to illness or injury.
- Often overlooked, but your ability to earn income is your most valuable financial asset.
- **Short-term disability** covers weeks to months. **Long-term disability** covers years or until retirement age.
- Check if your employer offers coverage. If not, consider an individual policy.

## How Much Coverage Do You Need?

### Life Insurance
A common guideline is 10-15 times your annual income, but consider:
- Outstanding debts (mortgage, student loans).
- Number of dependents and their ages.
- Future expenses like college tuition.
- Your spouse's income and ability to support the household.

### Health Insurance
Choose a plan that balances monthly premiums with out-of-pocket costs based on your expected healthcare usage. Healthy individuals may prefer high-deductible plans paired with a Health Savings Account (HSA).

### Disability Insurance
Aim for coverage that replaces at least 60% of your gross income. Review the policy's definition of "disability" and the waiting period before benefits begin.

## Key Takeaway

Insurance is not optional — it is a critical part of protecting your financial plan. Prioritize health insurance, get term life insurance if anyone depends on your income, and do not overlook disability coverage. The right insurance lets you take financial risks elsewhere with confidence.
