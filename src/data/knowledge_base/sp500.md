# Understanding the S&P 500

The S&P 500 (Standard & Poor's 500) is a stock market index that tracks the performance of 500 of the largest publicly traded companies in the United States. It is widely regarded as the single best gauge of the U.S. stock market and is the benchmark against which most investment managers measure their performance.

## How Companies Are Selected

The S&P 500 is not simply the 500 biggest companies by market value. A committee at S&P Dow Jones Indices selects companies based on several criteria:

- **Market capitalization:** Must be at least $14.5 billion (as of recent thresholds)
- **Liquidity:** Shares must be actively traded
- **Domicile:** Must be a U.S. company
- **Public float:** At least 50% of shares must be available to the public
- **Profitability:** Must have positive earnings in the most recent quarter and over the trailing four quarters
- **Sector representation:** The committee aims to reflect the broader U.S. economy

The index is **market-cap weighted**, meaning larger companies like Apple, Microsoft, and Amazon have a bigger influence on the index's performance than smaller companies.

## Historical Average Return

Over the long term, the S&P 500 has delivered an average annual return of approximately **10% before inflation** (about 7% after inflation). However, returns vary dramatically from year to year:

- **Best year (1995):** +34.1%
- **Worst year (2008):** -38.5%
- **Positive years:** The market has been positive in roughly 75% of calendar years

The 10% average includes both price appreciation and reinvested dividends. Missing even a few of the best trading days can significantly reduce your long-term returns, which is why staying invested matters more than timing the market.

## Why the S&P 500 Is a Benchmark

The S&P 500 serves as the standard benchmark for several reasons:

- It covers approximately **80% of the total U.S. stock market** by value
- It spans all 11 major market sectors (technology, healthcare, financials, etc.)
- It has a long, well-documented track record
- Most actively managed funds compare their performance against it

When financial news reports that "the market" went up or down, they are usually referring to the S&P 500.

## How to Invest in the S&P 500

You cannot buy the index directly, but you can invest in funds that track it:

### Index Mutual Funds
- Vanguard 500 Index Fund (VFIAX) – expense ratio 0.04%
- Fidelity 500 Index Fund (FXAIX) – expense ratio 0.015%
- Schwab S&P 500 Index Fund (SWPPX) – expense ratio 0.02%

### Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)
- SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY) – the oldest and most traded S&P 500 ETF
- Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO) – very low expense ratio of 0.03%
- iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV) – expense ratio of 0.03%

Both options give you exposure to all 500 companies in a single investment at very low cost.

## Key Takeaways

- The S&P 500 tracks 500 of the largest U.S. companies across all sectors
- Companies are selected by a committee based on size, liquidity, and profitability
- The historical average annual return is approximately 10% before inflation
- It is the most widely used benchmark for U.S. stock market performance
- You can invest in it easily through low-cost index funds or ETFs
