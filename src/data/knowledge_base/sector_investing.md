# Sector Investing

Sector investing involves focusing your investments on specific segments of the economy rather than the market as a whole. Understanding sectors helps you make more informed decisions about where to allocate your money and how to manage risk.

## The 11 GICS Sectors

The Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) divides the economy into 11 sectors:

1. **Information Technology** — Software, hardware, semiconductors (e.g., Apple, Microsoft)
2. **Health Care** — Pharmaceuticals, biotech, medical devices (e.g., Johnson & Johnson, Pfizer)
3. **Financials** — Banks, insurance, asset management (e.g., JPMorgan, Berkshire Hathaway)
4. **Consumer Discretionary** — Retail, autos, entertainment (e.g., Amazon, Tesla)
5. **Communication Services** — Telecom, media, social platforms (e.g., Alphabet, Meta)
6. **Industrials** — Aerospace, defense, machinery, transportation (e.g., Boeing, Caterpillar)
7. **Consumer Staples** — Food, beverages, household products (e.g., Procter & Gamble, Coca-Cola)
8. **Energy** — Oil, gas, renewable energy (e.g., ExxonMobil, Chevron)
9. **Utilities** — Electric, gas, and water services (e.g., Duke Energy, NextEra)
10. **Real Estate** — REITs and real estate services (e.g., Prologis, American Tower)
11. **Materials** — Chemicals, mining, packaging (e.g., Linde, Freeport-McMoRan)

## Cyclical vs. Defensive Sectors

Sectors behave differently depending on economic conditions:

- **Cyclical sectors** perform well when the economy is growing and struggle during downturns. Examples include Consumer Discretionary, Financials, Industrials, and Technology.
- **Defensive sectors** tend to hold up better during recessions because demand for their products remains steady. Examples include Consumer Staples, Health Care, and Utilities.

## Sector Rotation

Sector rotation is a strategy where investors shift money between sectors based on the economic cycle:

- **Early recovery** — Financials and Industrials often lead.
- **Mid-cycle expansion** — Technology and Consumer Discretionary tend to outperform.
- **Late cycle** — Energy and Materials may benefit from rising commodity prices.
- **Recession** — Defensive sectors like Utilities, Health Care, and Consumer Staples typically hold up best.

While the concept is sound, timing sector rotations accurately is extremely difficult. Most individual investors are better served by maintaining broad diversification.

## Sector ETFs

Exchange-traded funds make sector investing accessible and affordable. Popular sector ETF families include:

- **SPDR Select Sector ETFs** (e.g., XLK for Technology, XLF for Financials)
- **Vanguard Sector ETFs** (e.g., VGT for Information Technology)
- **iShares Sector ETFs** (e.g., IYH for Health Care)

## Concentration Risk

The biggest danger of sector investing is **concentration risk** — putting too much of your portfolio into one area. If that sector underperforms, your entire portfolio suffers. A balanced approach uses sector knowledge to tilt a diversified portfolio rather than making all-or-nothing bets on a single sector.
