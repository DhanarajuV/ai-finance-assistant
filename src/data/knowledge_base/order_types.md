# Understanding Order Types

When you buy or sell a stock, you choose an order type that tells your broker how and when to execute the trade. Understanding the main order types helps you trade with more control and confidence.

## Market Orders

A market order tells your broker to buy or sell immediately at the best available price.

- **Pros:** Fast execution. Your order is filled almost instantly during market hours.
- **Cons:** You do not control the exact price. In fast-moving markets, the price you get may differ from the price you saw — this is called slippage.
- **Best for:** Highly liquid stocks where the price is stable and you want to get in or out quickly.

## Limit Orders

A limit order sets the maximum price you are willing to pay (for a buy) or the minimum price you are willing to accept (for a sell).

- **Example:** You want to buy a stock currently trading at $52, but only if it drops to $50. You place a buy limit order at $50. The order only executes if the price reaches $50 or lower.
- **Pros:** You control the price. No surprises.
- **Cons:** Your order may never execute if the stock does not reach your limit price.
- **Best for:** When you have a specific price target and are willing to wait.

## Stop Orders (Stop-Loss Orders)

A stop order becomes a market order once the stock reaches a specified price, called the stop price.

- **Example:** You own a stock at $60 and want to limit your losses. You set a stop order at $55. If the price drops to $55, your stop order triggers and sells at the next available market price.
- **Pros:** Helps protect against large losses automatically.
- **Cons:** In a fast decline, the actual sale price may be below your stop price.
- **Best for:** Managing downside risk on positions you cannot monitor constantly.

## Understanding the Bid-Ask Spread

Every stock has two prices at any given moment:

- **Bid price:** The highest price a buyer is willing to pay.
- **Ask price:** The lowest price a seller is willing to accept.
- **Spread:** The difference between the bid and ask.

For heavily traded stocks like Apple, the spread might be just a penny. For thinly traded stocks, it could be much wider. A wider spread means higher trading costs for you.

## Market Hours

- **Regular trading hours:** 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM Eastern Time, Monday through Friday.
- **Pre-market:** Typically 4:00 AM to 9:30 AM ET. Lower volume and wider spreads.
- **After-hours:** Typically 4:00 PM to 8:00 PM ET. Same caveats as pre-market.

Limit orders are especially important during extended hours, when prices can be more volatile and spreads wider.

## Key Takeaway

Market orders prioritize speed, limit orders prioritize price, and stop orders help manage risk. Knowing when to use each type gives you better control over your trades and helps you avoid costly mistakes.
