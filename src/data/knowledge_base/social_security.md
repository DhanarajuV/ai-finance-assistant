# Understanding Social Security

Social Security is a federal program that provides retirement income, disability benefits, and survivor benefits. For most retirees, it forms a significant portion of their income. Understanding how benefits are calculated and when to claim can make a meaningful difference in your financial security.

## How Benefits Are Calculated

Your Social Security retirement benefit is based on your **35 highest-earning years**. Here is the basic process:

1. The Social Security Administration (SSA) adjusts your past earnings for wage inflation
2. Your 35 highest-earning years are averaged to determine your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)
3. A formula is applied to your AIME to calculate your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) — the benefit you receive at full retirement age

If you worked fewer than 35 years, zeros are averaged in for the missing years, which lowers your benefit. Working a few extra years can replace those zeros and increase your monthly payment.

## When to Claim: 62 vs. 67 vs. 70

You can start receiving benefits as early as age 62, but the age you claim permanently affects your monthly amount:

- **Age 62:** You receive a reduced benefit — roughly 30% less than your full retirement age amount
- **Age 67 (full retirement age for most):** You receive 100% of your calculated benefit
- **Age 70:** You receive the maximum benefit — about 24% more than your full retirement age amount

After age 70, there is no additional increase, so there is no financial reason to delay beyond 70.

### How to Decide

- **Claim early** if you need the income, have health concerns that may shorten your life expectancy, or want to reduce withdrawals from savings
- **Delay** if you are in good health, have other income sources to bridge the gap, and want the highest possible guaranteed monthly income

Each year you delay past full retirement age increases your benefit by approximately **8%** — a guaranteed return that is difficult to match elsewhere.

## Spousal Benefits

If you are married, you may be eligible for a spousal benefit equal to **up to 50%** of your spouse's full retirement age benefit. Key rules:

- You must be at least 62 to claim spousal benefits
- Your spouse must have already filed for their own benefits
- You receive the higher of your own benefit or the spousal benefit, not both
- Divorced spouses may also qualify if the marriage lasted at least 10 years

## How Social Security Fits Into Retirement Planning

Social Security is a foundation, not a complete plan. The average monthly benefit is roughly $1,900, which replaces about 40% of pre-retirement income for a median earner. To maintain your standard of living, you will likely need:

- Employer retirement plans (401(k), 403(b))
- Personal savings (IRA, taxable accounts)
- Other income sources (pensions, part-time work)

Create a free account at **ssa.gov** to view your estimated benefits based on your actual earnings history. This is one of the most useful steps you can take in retirement planning.
