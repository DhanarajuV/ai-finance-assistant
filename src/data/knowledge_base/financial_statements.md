# Understanding Financial Statements

Financial statements are the report cards of a business. Public companies are required to publish them regularly, and learning to read them gives you a significant edge when evaluating investments. There are three core financial statements every investor should understand.

## The Income Statement

The income statement shows how much money a company earned and spent over a specific period (a quarter or a year). It answers the question: **Is this company profitable?**

Key line items to know:

- **Revenue (Sales):** Total money earned from selling products or services.
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS):** Direct costs of producing what was sold.
- **Gross Profit:** Revenue minus COGS.
- **Operating Expenses:** Costs like salaries, rent, and marketing.
- **Operating Income:** Gross profit minus operating expenses. Reflects core business profitability.
- **Net Income:** The bottom line — profit after all expenses, interest, and taxes.

**Key metric:** Profit margin (Net Income / Revenue) tells you what percentage of each dollar in sales becomes profit.

## The Balance Sheet

The balance sheet is a snapshot of what a company owns and owes at a single point in time. It follows a fundamental equation:

**Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity**

- **Assets:** What the company owns — cash, inventory, property, equipment, and receivables.
- **Liabilities:** What the company owes — loans, accounts payable, and other debts.
- **Shareholders' Equity:** The difference between assets and liabilities, representing the owners' stake.

**Key metrics:**
- **Current Ratio** (Current Assets / Current Liabilities): Measures short-term ability to pay bills. Above 1.0 is generally healthy.
- **Debt-to-Equity Ratio** (Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity): Shows reliance on borrowed money. Lower is typically less risky.

## The Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement tracks the actual movement of cash in and out of the business. A company can report profits on the income statement but still run out of cash, which is why this statement matters.

It is divided into three sections:

- **Operating Activities:** Cash generated from the core business. Positive operating cash flow means the business generates real cash from its operations.
- **Investing Activities:** Cash spent on or received from long-term assets like equipment, property, or acquisitions.
- **Financing Activities:** Cash from issuing stock, borrowing, repaying debt, or paying dividends.

**Key metric:** Free Cash Flow (Operating Cash Flow minus Capital Expenditures) shows how much cash is available after maintaining and expanding the business.

## How the Three Statements Connect

- Net income from the income statement flows into the cash flow statement and the equity section of the balance sheet.
- Cash from the cash flow statement updates the cash line on the balance sheet.
- Together, they provide a complete picture of financial health, profitability, and cash generation.

## Key Takeaway

No single statement tells the whole story. Read all three together to understand whether a company is profitable, financially stable, and generating real cash.
