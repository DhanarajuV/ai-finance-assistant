# Index Funds Explained

An index fund is a type of mutual fund or ETF designed to replicate the performance of a specific market index. Instead of a manager picking individual stocks, the fund simply holds all (or a representative sample) of the securities in the index it tracks.

## How Index Funds Track an Index

A market index is a predefined list of securities that represents a segment of the market. For example, the S&P 500 index includes 500 of the largest U.S. companies. An S&P 500 index fund buys shares in all 500 of those companies in the same proportions as the index.

When the index changes its composition, the fund adjusts its holdings to match. This passive approach requires minimal decision-making, which keeps costs extremely low.

## Why Warren Buffett Recommends Them

Warren Buffett, one of the most successful investors in history, has repeatedly advised everyday investors to buy low-cost index funds. In his 2013 letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders, he wrote that his instructions for his estate trustee are to put 90% of the money into a very low-cost S&P 500 index fund.

His reasoning is straightforward:

- Most professional fund managers fail to beat the index over long periods
- High fees from active management drag down returns
- A low-cost index fund gives you broad market exposure with minimal effort
- Time in the market matters more than timing the market

Buffett even won a famous million-dollar bet that an S&P 500 index fund would outperform a collection of hedge funds over a 10-year period, and it did by a wide margin.

## The Low-Cost Advantage

Expense ratios for index funds are among the lowest in the investment world:

- **Fidelity Zero Total Market Index Fund (FZROX):** 0.00%
- **Vanguard 500 Index Fund (VFIAX):** 0.04%
- **Schwab S&P 500 Index Fund (SWPPX):** 0.02%

Compare that to the average actively managed fund charging 0.50%–1.00% or more.

### Example: The Impact of Fees
If you invest $10,000 with a 7% annual return over 30 years:
- At 0.04% fees: **~$74,017**
- At 1.00% fees: **~$57,435**

That is roughly $16,500 lost to fees alone on just a $10,000 investment.

## Popular Index Fund Examples

- **S&P 500 index funds** – track the 500 largest U.S. companies
- **Total stock market index funds** – cover virtually all publicly traded U.S. companies
- **International index funds** – track non-U.S. markets
- **Bond index funds** – track a broad bond market index
- **Total world index funds** – combine U.S. and international stocks in one fund

## Who Should Consider Index Funds?

Index funds are suitable for nearly any investor, but they are especially well-suited for beginners who want a simple hands-off approach, long-term investors saving for retirement, and anyone who wants broad diversification at minimal cost.

The simplicity of index funds is their greatest strength. You invest, stay the course, and let the market work for you over time.
