# Debt Management Strategies

Debt is a reality for most people, but not all debt is created equal. Understanding how to manage debt effectively is a critical part of building a strong financial foundation.

## Good Debt vs. Bad Debt

### Good Debt
Good debt is borrowing that helps you build wealth or increase your earning potential over time:

- **Mortgage:** Allows you to build equity in a home that may appreciate in value. Mortgage interest rates are typically low.
- **Student loans:** Can increase your earning potential if the degree leads to higher income. Federal student loans often have reasonable rates and flexible repayment options.
- **Business loans:** Borrowing to start or grow a business that generates income.

### Bad Debt
Bad debt is borrowing for things that lose value or do not generate returns:

- **Credit card debt:** Interest rates of 20-30% make this the most expensive common debt. Carrying a balance costs you significantly.
- **Auto loans on expensive cars:** Cars depreciate quickly. Borrowing heavily for a vehicle beyond your means is costly.
- **Payday loans:** Extremely high interest rates that can trap borrowers in a cycle of debt.

## Debt Payoff Strategies

### The Avalanche Method
Pay off debts in order of **highest interest rate first** while making minimum payments on everything else.

- **Advantage:** Saves the most money on interest over time.
- **Example:** If you have a credit card at 24% and a student loan at 5%, focus extra payments on the credit card first.

### The Snowball Method
Pay off debts in order of **smallest balance first** while making minimum payments on everything else.

- **Advantage:** Quick wins build motivation and momentum.
- **Example:** Pay off a $500 medical bill before tackling a $10,000 student loan, regardless of interest rates.

Both methods work. The avalanche method is mathematically optimal, but the snowball method can be more effective psychologically. Choose the one you will stick with.

## When to Pay Debt vs. Invest

This is one of the most common financial questions. A general framework:

- **Always make minimum payments** on all debts to protect your credit score.
- **High-interest debt (above 7-8%):** Prioritize paying this off. It is very difficult for investments to consistently earn more than 20%+ credit card interest.
- **Low-interest debt (below 4-5%):** Consider investing while making regular payments, since long-term market returns have historically exceeded these rates.
- **Employer 401(k) match:** Always contribute enough to get the full match — it is an immediate 50-100% return on your money.

## Credit Score Basics

Your credit score (typically 300-850) affects your ability to borrow and the interest rates you receive:

- **Payment history (35%):** Pay on time, every time. This is the biggest factor.
- **Credit utilization (30%):** Keep balances below 30% of your credit limit. Below 10% is ideal.
- **Length of credit history (15%):** Older accounts help your score. Avoid closing old cards.
- **Credit mix (10%):** Having different types of credit (cards, loans) helps modestly.
- **New credit inquiries (10%):** Applying for many accounts in a short period can lower your score temporarily.

## Key Takeaway

Eliminate high-interest debt as a top priority, use a payoff strategy you will stick with, and make informed decisions about when to pay debt versus invest. A strong credit score opens doors to better financial opportunities.
